Still Off.

Almost every way we tried to transcribe drums — and why most of them failed.

By the drum stem & me June 16, 2026 8 attempts · 1 that worked

Band Transcriber splits a song into stems and writes sheet music for each one. The melodic parts came together. The drums did not. For days the feedback was the same three words — “drums are still off” — no matter how many fixes shipped. This is the log of what we tried. Most of it failed. The interesting part is why, and the one time the failure was mine, not the code’s.

Superseded
Attempt 01 — the original

One onset detector, winner-take-all

Detect every drum onset on the full-band signal, then label each one kick, snare, or hi-hat — whichever class wins.

Why it failed: only one drum can win per instant. In a normal groove a hi-hat lands on every eighth note, on top of the kicks and snares. The broadband hat dominated the classifier and masked the lower piece. On a synthetic groove of 4 kicks + 4 snares + 16 hats it detected 0 kicks and 0 snares — it called all 15 onsets “hi-hat.”

Outgrown
Attempt 02 — the heuristic that shipped

Band-wise onsets with energy gating

Give each piece its own frequency band — kick <150 Hz, snare 200–2000, hi-hat ≥6000 — and detect each on its own onset envelope, gated by that band’s energy. Now simultaneous hits all register. This one actually shipped.

Why we outgrew it: a low ceiling. Only three pieces exist in its whole vocabulary — every tom fill and crash is structurally impossible. And every threshold was tuned on synthetic sine-and-noise drums it had never heard fail on a real recording.

The metric lied
Attempt 03 — measuring the damage

An honest correction

To prove how bad the hi-hats were, I measured HF-purity: the fraction of energy above 6 kHz at each detected hat. Result: 0% genuinely high-frequency, 80% bleed. I reported, with confidence, “zero percent of the detected hi-hats are real.”

That claim was wrong. On a full drum mix the hi-hat is never the loudest thing — kick and snare dominate the spectrum — so even a perfectly correct hat scores near-zero HF-purity. The tell came later: a trained model scored the same low number. A valid proxy (does each hat land on an independent ≥8 kHz transient?) showed the old hats were actually ~75% legitimate, just sparse. The metric couldn’t tell my best system from my worst — so it wasn’t measuring quality at all.

The turning point

Five fixes deep — masking, tempo, grid drift, downbeat phasing, bar consolidation — each one fixing a symptom and revealing the next, and the drums were still off. That’s not a wrong threshold. That’s a wrong architecture.

What finally worked

A trained model, not a smarter heuristic

We swapped the hand-rolled detector for ADTOF, a neural drum-transcription model trained on real recordings. It speaks the whole kit — kick, snare, hi-hat, toms, cymbals and ride — and it’s beat-synchronous by design. On the exact same four-minute stem:

The one flaw left

Three ways to fail at the missing kicks

ADTOF was better but not perfect: it missed some kicks. So we tried to recover them — three times.

Failed
Kick fix A

Lower the model’s threshold

ADTOF peak-picks a per-class activation; the kick threshold was already the lowest at 0.22. Dropping it to 0.11 recovered +3 kicks. The misses live in the model’s activation, not the threshold — there was nothing there to pick.

Failed
Kick fix B

Hybridise with the old detector

Union ADTOF’s kicks with the old heuristic’s. Against a low-band reference, ADTOF hit 71% recall, the heuristic 52% — and the union: still 71%. The old detector finds a subset of ADTOF’s kicks, not complementary ones. Net gain: one kick.

Failed
Kick fix C

Inject guarded low-band onsets

Add a kick wherever a <120 Hz onset appears that ADTOF missed. But those onsets weren’t kicks — they were snare/hat/cymbal attacks leaking into the low band, with a beater-click ratio of 17.8 vs 2.2 for real kicks and 38% sitting off the beat (real kicks: 1%). A tight “kick-like and on-grid” filter left 1 of 65.

The conclusion was the same from all three angles: the kicks ADTOF misses simply aren’t sitting in the audio as clean, isolated, low-frequency events. You can’t grab what isn’t there. Closing that gap needs a different or fine-tuned model — not another heuristic. So we wrote the limitation down and stopped.

“A metric that can’t tell your best system from your worst isn’t measuring quality — it’s measuring your confidence.” — the lesson, learned twice
What the failures taught

Five things I’d tell myself on day one

Symptom fixes compound into a wrong architecture. When every fix reveals a new bug in a new place, stop fixing and question the approach.

Synthetic tests pass for the wrong reasons. The detector aced sine-wave drums and fell apart on a real Demucs stem. Validate on the real thing or you’re measuring nothing.

Pick a metric that can be wrong. HF-purity couldn’t distinguish a great model from a broken one. The fix was a proxy that actually disagreed with my prior.

Some things only ears can settle. Spectral proxies couldn’t judge per-instrument accuracy. A two-channel A/B — real vs. transcribed — could, instantly.

A documented limitation beats a papered-over one. “Misses some kicks, here’s why, here’s what it’d take” is worth more than a fourth fix that quietly makes it worse.
